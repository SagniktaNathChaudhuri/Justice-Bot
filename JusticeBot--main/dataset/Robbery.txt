1. Indian Penal Code (IPC), 1860: Provisions Related to Robbery
Section 378 – Theft
Definition: Theft is defined as dishonestly taking any movable property out of the possession of any person without that person's consent with the intention to take it.
Section 379 – Punishment for Theft
Punishment: Imprisonment which may extend to three years, or with a fine, or both.
Section 390 – Definition of Robbery
Definition: Robbery is essentially an aggravated form of theft or extortion.
Theft becomes robbery when, in order to commit the theft or while committing the theft or while carrying away or attempting to carry away property obtained by theft, the offender voluntarily causes or attempts to cause to any person death or hurt or wrongful restraint, or fear of instant death or instant hurt, or instant wrongful restraint.
Extortion becomes robbery when the offender, at the time of committing the extortion, is in the presence of the person put in fear, and commits extortion by putting that person in fear of instant death, instant hurt, or instant wrongful restraint to that person or to some other person, and, by so putting in fear, induces the person so put in fear then and there to deliver up the thing extorted.
Section 392 – Punishment for Robbery
Punishment: Imprisonment for a term which may extend to ten years and shall also be liable to a fine.
Exception: If the robbery is committed on a highway between sunset and sunrise, the imprisonment may extend to fourteen years.
Section 393 – Attempt to Commit Robbery
Punishment: Imprisonment for a term which may extend to seven years, and shall also be liable to a fine.
Section 394 – Voluntarily Causing Hurt in Committing Robbery
Definition: If any person, while committing or attempting to commit robbery, voluntarily causes hurt to any person, such offender shall be punished.
Punishment: Imprisonment for life or with rigorous imprisonment for a term which may extend to ten years, and shall also be liable to a fine.
Section 395 – Dacoity
Definition: Dacoity is committed when five or more persons conjointly commit or attempt to commit robbery.
Punishment: Imprisonment for life or with rigorous imprisonment for a term which may extend to ten years, and shall also be liable to a fine.
Section 396 – Dacoity with Murder
Definition: If any one of the five or more persons committing dacoity commits murder in so committing dacoity, every one of those persons shall be punished.
Punishment: Punishable with death, or imprisonment for life, or rigorous imprisonment for a term which may extend to ten years, and shall also be liable to a fine.
Section 397 – Robbery or Dacoity, with Attempt to Cause Death or Grievous Hurt
Definition: If, at the time of committing robbery or dacoity, the offender uses any deadly weapon, or causes grievous hurt to any person, or attempts to cause death or grievous hurt to any person, the punishment is severe.
Punishment: Rigorous imprisonment for not less than seven years.
Section 398 – Attempt to Commit Robbery or Dacoity When Armed with Deadly Weapon
Definition: If at the time of attempting to commit robbery or dacoity, the offender is armed with any deadly weapon, they shall be punished.
Punishment: Rigorous imprisonment for not less than seven years.
2. Key Legal Procedures Under the Code of Criminal Procedure (CrPC), 1973
The Code of Criminal Procedure (CrPC), 1973, provides the procedural framework for the investigation, trial, and punishment of offenses like robbery.

Investigation
Filing of FIR (First Information Report): Under Section 154 of the CrPC, when the police receive information about the commission of a cognizable offense like robbery, they are required to register an FIR and commence an investigation.
Investigation Process: The police investigate the crime scene, collect evidence, record witness statements under Section 161, and may arrest suspects without a warrant if they are caught in the act or there is substantial evidence against them.
Trial and Procedure
Sessions Court: Robbery and dacoity cases are generally tried in a Sessions Court due to the severity of the offense.
Framing of Charges: The court will frame charges based on the evidence presented. If the accused pleads not guilty, the case proceeds to trial.
Examination of Evidence and Witnesses: Both the prosecution and defense present their evidence, including witness testimonies and documents.
Arguments and Judgment: The final arguments are presented by both parties, and the court delivers a judgment based on the evidence and testimonies.
Appeals
Right to Appeal: The accused has the right to appeal the decision of the Sessions Court to the High Court under Section 374 of the CrPC, and further to the Supreme Court if necessary.
3. Indian Evidence Act, 1872: Rules of Evidence in Robbery Cases
The Indian Evidence Act, 1872 lays down the rules for admissibility of evidence in robbery cases.

Types of Evidence
Direct Evidence: This includes witness testimonies who have directly observed the crime.
Circumstantial Evidence: This can include evidence like fingerprints, DNA, or CCTV footage linking the accused to the crime.
Confessions: Confessions made by the accused, under certain conditions, can be admitted as evidence. However, confessions made to police officers are not admissible unless made in the presence of a Magistrate.
Recoveries: The recovery of stolen property from the accused or at their instance can be crucial evidence in robbery cases.
